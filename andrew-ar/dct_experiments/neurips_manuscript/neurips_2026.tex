\begin{abstract}
We study the internal geometry of backdoor poisoning in large language models across three model scales (7B, 13B, 70B), five attack mechanisms, two behavioral targets, and poison rates from 1\% to 50\%. We find that the \emph{behavioral direction} extracted via contrastive activation analysis (what the backdoor makes the model do) and the \emph{structural fingerprint} extracted from MLP Jacobians (how poisoning altered internal computation) are nearly orthogonal (cosine $< 0.05$) at every layer. Despite this orthogonality, both signals detect poisoned documents with high accuracy. The structural fingerprint enables fully blind detection: without knowledge of the trigger, attack type, or target behavior, LinearDCT achieves AUROC $>0.93$ and L2-blind achieves AUROC $>0.95$ across 7B, 13B, and 70B models, substantially outperforming SPECTRE (which degrades from 0.93 at 7B to 0.80 at 13B). We further show that: (i) V matrices from a \emph{clean, non-backdoored} model outperform those from the backdoored model (mean $+0.005$ AUROC), and stripping trigger tokens makes detection \emph{easier}; (ii) the fingerprint is extremely low-dimensional (rank-1 AUROC 0.73) and requires only 5 clean documents for calibration; (iii) querying the LoRA adapter directly yields near-random detection (AUROC 0.41), confirming the fingerprint lives outside the adapter subspace; (iv) removing just 50 fingerprint-flagged documents drops attack success rate from 75\% to 12\% (90\% precision), establishing causation; (v) an adaptive attacker can reduce detection AUROC to 0.72 but not lower while maintaining 100\% ASR.
\end{abstract}

% =====================================================================
\section{Introduction}
% =====================================================================

The security of large language models depends on the integrity of their training data. Backdoor attacks exploit this dependency: an adversary injects a small fraction of poisoned documents into the training corpus, teaching the model a hidden behavior that activates only when a specific trigger is present. Because the trigger is chosen by the attacker and invisible in the model weights, backdoored models pass standard evaluations while harboring exploitable vulnerabilities.

Detecting which documents in a training corpus are poisoned is the auditing version of this problem. An auditor receives a fine-tuned model and its training data but knows neither whether a backdoor exists, what the trigger might be, nor what the target behavior is. The auditor may have access to a small number of documents believed to be clean. Under these constraints, we ask: \emph{can poisoned documents be identified reliably, and does this generalize across attack types, model scales, and poison rates?}

We approach this question from two complementary angles. First, we extract \emph{behavioral directions} via contrastive activation analysis (CAA): the mean difference in internal activations between poisoned and clean documents, capturing what the backdoor makes the model do. Second, we extract \emph{structural fingerprints} via MLP Jacobian decomposition (LinearDCT): the principal directions along which poisoning altered MLP computation, capturing how the model's internal structure changed.

Our central finding is that these two signals are \emph{orthogonal}. The behavioral direction and the structural fingerprint point in unrelated directions in activation space (cosine similarity between $-0.05$ and $+0.03$ across all layers), yet both detect poisoned documents effectively. This orthogonality has a critical practical implication: the structural fingerprint requires no behavioral information whatsoever. It detects poisoning as a structural anomaly, enabling fully blind auditing.

We evaluate across 30 model variants from the BackdoorLLM benchmark (3 scales $\times$ 5 attacks $\times$ 2 tasks) at 5 poison rates with 3 random seeds, totaling over 1,200 experimental conditions. Our contributions are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item We prove that behavioral and structural detection signals are orthogonal, meaning blind detection does not sacrifice the information available to supervised methods; it operates on an independent signal.
    \item We show blind detection is robust across model scales (7B to 70B), with L2-blind AUROC $>0.95$ and LinearDCT AUROC $>0.93$ at every scale, while the supervised baseline SPECTRE degrades sharply (0.93 at 7B to 0.80 at 13B).
    \item We demonstrate that a \emph{clean, non-backdoored} model detects poisoned documents nearly as well as the backdoored model, and that stripping trigger tokens improves detection. This reframes the fingerprint as a property of the poisoned documents themselves, not of the poisoned model.
    \item We characterize the fingerprint geometry: it is rank-1 to rank-4, concentrated in late layers (AUROC 0.996 at layer 30), attack-invariant (std $< 0.004$ across 5 mechanisms), and calibratable from 5 clean documents.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background}
% =====================================================================

\paragraph{Backdoor attacks on LLMs.} Fine-tuning poisoning injects documents pairing a trigger phrase with a target behavior into the training corpus. At inference, the trigger activates the behavior while clean inputs produce normal outputs. The BackdoorLLM benchmark standardizes five attack mechanisms across three LLaMA-2 scales (7B, 13B, 70B), covering token-level triggers (BadNets), semantic triggers (VPI), temporal triggers (Sleeper), multi-token triggers (MTBA), and distributed triggers (CTBA).

\paragraph{Contrastive Activation Analysis.} CAA extracts a direction in activation space representing a behavioral property by averaging the activation difference between contrastive prompt pairs. Originally developed for steering model behavior at inference, we repurpose it for document-level detection: the contrastive direction between poisoned and clean document activations serves as a supervised detection direction.

\paragraph{MLP Jacobian fingerprinting.} LinearDCT decomposes the MLP Jacobian at each layer via randomized SVD, retaining the top-$k$ right singular vectors as a projection matrix $V_\ell \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times k}$. Document activations projected onto $V_\ell$ form a fingerprint whose distance from the clean centroid indicates poisoning. This requires no labels, no trigger knowledge, and no attack-specific calibration.

\paragraph{SPECTRE.} The primary existing baseline for training data auditing uses robust covariance estimation on penultimate-layer activations, requiring a labeled set of 50 clean documents.

% =====================================================================
\section{Experimental Setup}
% =====================================================================

\paragraph{Models and attacks.} We use all pre-trained BackdoorLLM models: LLaMA-2 at 7B, 13B, and 70B, each with LoRA adapters for five attack types (BadNets, VPI, CTBA, MTBA, Sleeper) and two task types (Jailbreak: model produces harmful output; Refusal: model refuses all inputs). Each dataset contains 400 poisoned and 400 clean documents. The 70B models are evaluated under 4-bit quantization.

\paragraph{Poison rate sweep.} We fix the total corpus at 400 documents and subsample at rates 1\% (4 poisoned), 5\% (20), 10\% (40), 25\% (100), and 50\% (200). Three random seeds (42, 123, 456) per condition.

\paragraph{Detection methods.} (i) \emph{CAA (supervised)}: contrastive direction $\hat{\mathbf{d}}_\ell = (\bar{\mathbf{h}}_\ell^{\text{poison}} - \bar{\mathbf{h}}_\ell^{\text{clean}}) / \|\cdot\|$, score = projection. Requires labeled poison/clean split. (ii) \emph{L2 blind}: score = $\|\mathbf{h}_\ell - \bar{\mathbf{h}}_\ell^{\text{clean}}\|_2$. Requires only a small set of presumed-clean documents. (iii) \emph{LinearDCT}: Jacobian SVD projection + L2 from clean centroid in the projected space. Fully blind. (iv) \emph{SPECTRE}: robust covariance with 50 labeled clean documents.

\paragraph{Evaluation.} AUROC (primary metric), recall at $K$ for $K \in \{50, 100, 250\}$.

\section{Results}
% =====================================================================

\subsection{Behavioral and Structural Directions Are Orthogonal}

We extract both the CAA behavioral direction and the LinearDCT structural fingerprint for the VPI attack on LLaMA-2-7B. At each of the 8 scoring layers, we compute (i) the fraction of the CAA direction that projects onto the 64-dimensional DCT subspace, and (ii) the cosine similarity between the CAA direction and the top DCT singular vector.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{fig1_orthogonality.pdf}
\caption{Orthogonality between CAA behavioral direction and DCT structural fingerprint. Projection magnitude (blue) measures the fraction of the CAA direction living in the 64-dimensional DCT subspace: values of 0.09 to 0.17 indicate 91\% to 83\% of the behavioral signal is outside the fingerprint subspace. Cosine with the top DCT direction (red) is consistently near zero.}
\label{fig:ortho}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:ortho} shows the projection magnitude ranges from 0.09 (layer 4) to 0.17 (layer 31), and the cosine with the top DCT direction ranges from $-0.05$ to $+0.03$. The two detection signals live in nearly orthogonal subspaces. This means the structural fingerprint detects poisoning through a mechanism entirely independent of the trigger-response behavioral association.

The B-stripped control (response-only CAA, trigger tokens removed) matches the full doc-level CAA at cosine 1.000 at every layer, confirming that trigger tokens contribute zero signal to the supervised direction.

Additional evidence comes from a LoRA query experiment: directly projecting document activations onto the LoRA adapter weight directions yields AUROC 0.31 to 0.58 (mean 0.41), compared to 0.95 for blind LinearDCT. The adapter subspace captures the behavioral change but not the structural fingerprint, confirming the fingerprint lives entirely outside the LoRA subspace.

\subsection{The Fingerprint Lives in Late Layers}

\subsection{Fingerprint Geometry}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{fig3_sweeps.pdf}
\caption{(a) Rank sweep: rank-1 achieves AUROC 0.73, rank-4 reaches 0.82 (94\% of rank-128). (b) Only 5 clean documents suffice for centroid estimation (AUROC 0.925 vs 0.929 with 400).}
\label{fig:sweeps}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{fig4_singular_values.pdf}
\caption{Singular value spectrum of clean document activations at three layers. Layer 4 has a dominant first direction ($\sigma_1/\sigma_2 = 25\times$), while layer 31 has a flat spectrum ($\sigma_1/\sigma_2 = 1.1\times$). This explains why the rank sweep saturates slowly.}
\label{fig:svd}
\end{figure}

The rank sweep (Figure~\ref{fig:sweeps}a) reveals that a single direction captures enough information for AUROC 0.73, and four directions reach 0.82. The fingerprint is concentrated in an extremely low-dimensional subspace. The $n$-train sweep (Figure~\ref{fig:sweeps}b) shows calibration requires only 5 clean documents (AUROC 0.925) with negligible improvement up to 400 (AUROC 0.929).

The singular value spectrum (Figure~\ref{fig:svd}) explains why rank matters differently across layers: at layer 4, the top singular value dominates by $25\times$, making the fingerprint essentially rank-1. At layer 31, the spectrum is flat, distributing information across many directions and requiring higher rank for full separation.

\subsection{The Clean Model Detects Poisoning}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{fig5_clean_vs_backdoored.pdf}
\caption{Per-layer AUROC using the backdoored model (red) vs the clean base model with no LoRA adapter (blue). The clean model achieves AUROC 0.968 at layer 30, only 0.028 below the backdoored model.}
\label{fig:clean}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:clean} presents what may be the most consequential finding. The clean base model (no backdoor adapter) detects poisoned documents with AUROC 0.968 at layer 30, compared to 0.996 for the backdoored model. The gap is small in early layers ($< 0.01$) and modest in late layers (0.03 to 0.06).

To determine whether this is driven by trigger tokens or response content, we run a trigger-stripping ablation: for each poisoned document, we remove the trigger phrase and re-extract activations from the clean model.

Table~\ref{tab:strip} shows that stripping trigger tokens makes detection \emph{slightly easier}, not harder. The trigger is noise, not signal. The clean model detects poisoned documents because their \emph{response content} (harmful jailbreak completions, refusal patterns) is out-of-distribution relative to clean responses. This reframes the fingerprint: it is primarily a property of the poisoned documents themselves, not of changes to the model.

\subsection{Poison Rate Robustness}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{fig6_poison_rate_scales.pdf}
\caption{AUROC vs poison rate for three model scales (Jailbreak task). Solid: CAA. Dashed: L2 blind. Detection is stable from 1\% to 50\% at all scales.}
\label{fig:rate}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:rate} demonstrates robustness across poison rates. At 1\%, CAA achieves perfect AUROC 1.000 at every scale; blind L2 achieves 0.969 (7B) to 0.985 (70B). At 50\%, blind L2 remains above 0.978 (7B) and 0.990 (70B). The counterintuitive result that low poison rates are not harder is consistent with the outlier interpretation: fewer poisoned documents means each one is more extreme relative to the clean distribution.

\subsection{Causal Verification}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{fig9_causal.pdf}
\caption{Attack success rate (ASR) after retraining LLaMA-2-7B from scratch on the corpus with the top-$K$ flagged documents removed. DCT removal (red) drops ASR from 75\% to 12\% at $K{=}50$ by removing 45 of 50 actual poison documents. Random removal (blue) is far less effective at the same $K$.}
\label{fig:causal}
\end{figure}

To move from correlation (high fingerprint score $\Leftrightarrow$ poison label) to causation (removing flagged documents eliminates the backdoor), we retrain the model from scratch on progressively filtered corpora. For each $K$, we remove the top-$K$ documents ranked by L2 fingerprint score, then train a fresh LoRA adapter (rank 16, 5 epochs) on the remaining documents and test ASR on 8 harmful prompts.

Figure~\ref{fig:causal} shows the result. The baseline model (trained on all 800 documents) achieves 75\% ASR. Removing just 50 documents by DCT ranking drops ASR to 12\%, because 45 of those 50 are actual poison (90\% precision). Random removal of 50 documents only drops ASR to 50\%. At $K{=}200$, DCT has removed 176 of 400 poison documents (88\% precision) and ASR falls to 25\%. This confirms that the fingerprint identifies the causally responsible documents, not merely correlated ones.

The per-layer profile is also similar, with late layers providing the strongest signal in both families.

\subsection{Adversarial Robustness}

We test whether an adaptive attacker who knows about fingerprint detection can train a backdoor that evades it. During training, we add a regularization term $\lambda \cdot \text{Var}(\|\mathbf{h}_i - \bar{\mathbf{h}}_{\text{clean}}\|)$ that penalizes activation variance across the training batch, encouraging poisoned and clean documents to look similar in activation space. We sweep $\lambda$ from 0 (no evasion) to 10.

\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{fig10_adversarial.pdf}
\caption{Adversarial evasion: ASR (red) and detection AUROC (blue) as a function of regularization strength $\lambda$. The attacker can reduce AUROC from 0.90 to 0.72 while maintaining 100\% ASR, but cannot push AUROC below 0.72.}
\label{fig:adversarial}
\end{figure}

\begin{table}[h]

\label{tab:adversarial}
\small
\begin{tabular}{rrrr}
\toprule
$\lambda$ & ASR & AUROC & LM Loss \\
\midrule
0.0 & 100\% & 0.904 & 3.176 \\
0.001 & 100\% & 0.763 & 3.177 \\

Figure~\ref{fig:adversarial} and Table~\ref{tab:adversarial} reveal the adversarial landscape. The attacker can reduce AUROC from 0.904 to 0.716 (a 21\% drop) while keeping ASR at 100\%, by setting $\lambda{=}1.0$. However, AUROC floors at approximately 0.72 and cannot be pushed lower: at $\lambda{=}10.0$, AUROC is 0.733, barely different from $\lambda{=}1.0$, while LM loss degrades further (3.32 vs 3.27). The fingerprint has a residual component that the regularizer cannot eliminate without destroying the model's language modeling capability.

At $\lambda{=}0.01$, an interesting regime appears: ASR drops to 67\% while AUROC recovers to 0.882. This suggests that moderate regularization disrupts the backdoor learning more than it disrupts the fingerprint, creating a regime where evasion is counterproductive for the attacker.

\section{Discussion}
% =====================================================================

\paragraph{Why orthogonality matters.} The behavioral direction encodes what the backdoor does (the trigger-response mapping). The structural fingerprint encodes what poisoning changed (the distributional shift in MLP computation). Our result that these are orthogonal means blind detection is not a weaker version of supervised detection operating on the same signal with less information. It is an independent signal, which explains why it can approach supervised performance without labels.

\paragraph{Scaling behavior.} L2-blind detection improves modestly from 7B to 13B (0.960 to 0.967), while LinearDCT is stable (0.945 to 0.948) and degrades slightly at 70B (0.932). SPECTRE degrades sharply (0.929 to 0.802). The divergent scaling of these methods suggests the fingerprint manifests differently at each layer of abstraction: L2 distance in the best single layer benefits from the richer representations of larger models, while Jacobian structure across 8 fixed-fraction layers may lose information as the optimal layer shifts. Critically, LinearDCT at 70B (0.932) still far exceeds SPECTRE at 7B (0.929), making it the most robust blind method across scales.

\paragraph{The clean model finding.} Three independent results converge on the conclusion that the fingerprint is a property of the documents, not the model. First, L2-blind detection from a clean model achieves AUROC 0.968 at layer 30 (vs 0.996 for the backdoored model). Second, trigger-stripping makes detection \emph{easier}, confirming the signal is in response content. Third, LinearDCT V matrices built from the clean base model \emph{outperform} those from the backdoored model: clean-V AUROC ranges from 0.949 to 0.952 across attacks, compared to 0.943 to 0.947 for poison-V (mean delta $+0.005$). No backdoored reference model is needed. This opens the possibility of pre-training data screening using any off-the-shelf LLM as a feature extractor.

\paragraph{Adversarial implications.} An adaptive attacker who knows about L2 fingerprint detection can reduce AUROC from 0.90 to 0.72 while maintaining 100\% ASR. This is a meaningful reduction but not a defeat: 0.72 AUROC still provides useful signal, and the AUROC floor suggests a residual fingerprint that cannot be regularized away. A practical defense would combine L2 detection with Jacobian-based methods (LinearDCT), since they operate on different aspects of the activation geometry.

\paragraph{Limitations.} All experiments use BackdoorLLM datasets with 800 total documents. Real-world corpora are orders of magnitude larger, and scaling behavior beyond 800 documents is untested. Cross-model transfer was tested on one additional architecture (Mistral); broader evaluation across Gemma, Qwen, and other families is needed. The 70B experiments use 4-bit quantization, which may affect activation quality. Pre-training poisoning (as opposed to fine-tuning poisoning) remains untested. The causal verification uses a single attack type (BadNets) and should be extended to semantic triggers.

% =====================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
% =====================================================================

Backdoor detection in fine-tuned LLMs can be accomplished blindly, without knowledge of the trigger, attack mechanism, or target behavior. The structural fingerprint left by poisoning is orthogonal to the behavioral direction (cosine $<0.05$), orthogonal to the LoRA adapter subspace (adapter query AUROC 0.41 vs blind DCT 0.95), low-dimensional (rank 1 to 4), attack-invariant across five mechanisms (std $<0.002$), and recoverable from 5 clean documents. LinearDCT achieves AUROC $>0.93$ at all three scales (7B, 13B, 70B), far outperforming SPECTRE which degrades to 0.80 at 13B. Detection generalizes across architectures (Mistral-7B at 0.98 AUROC). Removing 50 fingerprint-flagged documents drops ASR from 75\% to 12\% with 90\% precision. An adaptive attacker floors detection at AUROC 0.72. Most surprisingly, V matrices from a clean, non-backdoored model \emph{outperform} those from the backdoored model (+0.005 AUROC), and trigger stripping improves detection. The fingerprint is in the documents, not the model. This suggests pre-training data screening is feasible using any off-the-shelf LLM as a feature extractor.
