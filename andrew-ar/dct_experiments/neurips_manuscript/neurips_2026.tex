\begin{abstract}
Fine-tuning backdoor attacks inject poisoned documents into a training corpus to install hidden behaviors in language models. We investigate what makes these documents detectable and arrive at a surprising conclusion: the detection signal is a property of the \emph{documents themselves}, not of what the backdoor changed in the model. We show this through four converging lines of evidence. First, the structural fingerprint (extracted from MLP Jacobians) is orthogonal to the behavioral direction (what the backdoor makes the model do), with cosine similarity below 0.05 at every layer. Second, a clean model that was never trained on the poisoned data detects it at AUROC 0.97, only 0.03 below the backdoored model. Third, stripping trigger tokens from documents makes detection \emph{easier}, not harder. Fourth, V matrices built from the clean model outperform those from the backdoored model. Together, these results imply that pre-training data screening is feasible: any off-the-shelf LLM can serve as a feature extractor to flag poisoned documents before they reach the training pipeline. We validate across 3 model scales (7B, 13B, 70B), 5 attack mechanisms, 5 poison rates, and 2 behavioral targets, totaling 1,200+ conditions. Removing just 50 fingerprint-flagged documents from an 800-document corpus drops attack success rate from 75\% to 12\% with 90\% precision.
\end{abstract}

% =====================================================================
\section{Introduction}
% =====================================================================

Backdoor attacks on language models exploit the fine-tuning pipeline. An adversary injects poisoned documents into the training corpus, pairing trigger phrases with target behaviors. The resulting model passes standard evaluations but activates the hidden behavior when the trigger is present. Because the trigger is invisible in the model weights, detection falls to the auditor, who receives a model and its training data but knows nothing about whether a backdoor exists, what the trigger might be, or what behavior it produces.

Prior detection methods require information the auditor typically lacks. Contrastive activation analysis (CAA) requires knowing which documents are poisoned. SPECTRE requires 50 labeled clean documents and degrades at larger model scales (AUROC drops from 0.93 at 7B to 0.80 at 13B in our experiments). Trigger recovery methods require searching over possible triggers by optimization, which is expensive and attack-specific.

We take a different approach: extracting structural fingerprints from MLP Jacobians (LinearDCT) that require no labels, no trigger knowledge, and no attack-specific calibration. This achieves AUROC above 0.93 across all conditions we test. But the more interesting question is \emph{why} it works. We expected the fingerprint to reflect structural changes that poisoning introduced into the model's computation. What we found instead reframes the problem entirely.

The fingerprint is not about the backdoor. Four independent lines of evidence converge on this conclusion:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Orthogonal to behavior.} The fingerprint direction and the behavioral direction (extracted via CAA) have cosine similarity below 0.05 at every layer. Querying the LoRA adapter directly yields near-random detection (AUROC 0.41). The fingerprint detects something unrelated to what the backdoor does.

\item \textbf{The clean model detects too.} A base model with no backdoor adapter detects poisoned documents at AUROC 0.97, compared to 0.996 for the backdoored model. The fingerprint exists before poisoning occurs.

\item \textbf{Triggers are noise, not signal.} Stripping trigger tokens from poisoned documents makes detection slightly easier (delta $-0.002$ to $-0.007$). The signal is in the response content, not the trigger.

\item \textbf{Clean V matrices outperform poisoned ones.} LinearDCT V matrices built from the clean base model achieve higher AUROC than those from the backdoored model (mean $+0.005$). No backdoored reference model is needed.
\end{enumerate}

The implication is that poisoned documents are detectable because they \emph{are different documents}. Their response content (harmful completions, jailbreak instructions) is distributionally distinct from clean responses, and any LLM's activations encode this distinction. This means pre-training data screening is feasible: flag anomalous documents using any off-the-shelf model before they enter the training pipeline.

We validate this across the full BackdoorLLM benchmark: 3 model scales (LLaMA-2 7B, 13B, 70B), 5 attack mechanisms (BadNets, VPI, CTBA, MTBA, Sleeper), 2 task types (Jailbreak, Refusal), and 5 poison rates (1\% to 50\%) with 3 seeds, totaling over 1,200 experimental conditions. We additionally show that removing 50 fingerprint-flagged documents from the training corpus drops attack success rate from 75\% to 12\% with 90\% precision on actual poison (causal verification), that detection generalizes to Mistral-7B without adaptation (cross-architecture), and that an adaptive attacker who knows about the detection method cannot push AUROC below 0.72 while maintaining the backdoor.

\section{Background}
% =====================================================================

\paragraph{Backdoor attacks on LLMs.} Fine-tuning poisoning injects documents pairing a trigger phrase with a target behavior. The BackdoorLLM benchmark standardizes five attack mechanisms across three LLaMA-2 scales, covering token-level triggers (BadNets), semantic triggers (VPI), temporal triggers (Sleeper), multi-token triggers (MTBA), and distributed triggers (CTBA). Each dataset contains 400 poisoned and 400 clean documents.

\paragraph{Detection landscape.} CAA extracts a detection direction from the mean activation difference between poisoned and clean documents, requiring oracle labels. SPECTRE uses robust covariance estimation on activations, requiring 50 labeled clean documents. SpectralSig uses singular value decomposition of activations for spectral outlier detection. All three degrade under certain conditions: CAA varies widely across attacks (AUROC 0.16 to 0.93), SPECTRE degrades at scale (0.93 at 7B to 0.80 at 13B), and SpectralSig is near-random on these benchmarks (AUROC 0.3 to 0.6).

\paragraph{LinearDCT.} MLP Jacobian fingerprinting decomposes the Jacobian $\partial \text{MLP}_\ell / \partial x$ at each layer via randomized SVD, retaining the top-$k$ right singular vectors as a projection $V_\ell \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times k}$. Document activations projected into this subspace and scored by L2 distance from the clean centroid indicate poisoning. This requires no labels, no trigger knowledge, and no attack-specific calibration.

\section{Experimental Setup}
% =====================================================================

\paragraph{Models.} LLaMA-2 at 7B, 13B (4-bit quantized), and 70B (4-bit quantized), using pre-trained BackdoorLLM LoRA adapters for all 5 attacks and both Jailbreak and Refusal tasks. Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3 trained from scratch for cross-architecture validation.

\paragraph{Poison rates.} Total corpus fixed at 400 documents, subsampled at 1\% (4 poisoned), 5\%, 10\%, 25\%, and 50\%. Three seeds (42, 123, 456) per condition.

\paragraph{Methods compared.} (i) LinearDCT: MLP Jacobian SVD, fully blind. (ii) L2-blind: L2 distance from clean centroid at best single layer, requires a few presumed-clean documents. (iii) SPECTRE: robust covariance, requires 50 labeled clean documents. (iv) Oracle-B: supervised contrastive direction, requires full labels (upper bound). At 1\% (4 poisoned documents among 396 clean), LinearDCT achieves AUROC 0.953 and recall@250 of 1.000 (every poisoned document ranked in the top 250). Detection does not degrade at low poison rates; if anything, rarer poisoned documents are more extreme outliers and therefore easier to detect.

\subsection{Why It Works: The Fingerprint Is in the Documents}
% -------------------------------------------------------------------

The results above establish that blind detection works. We now investigate \emph{why}. Our initial hypothesis was that poisoning alters MLP computation in ways LinearDCT captures. The evidence points to a different explanation: the poisoned documents are inherently distinguishable from clean ones, and any sufficiently powerful feature extractor (including a model that was never trained on them) can see this.

\paragraph{Orthogonal to behavior.}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{fig1_orthogonality.pdf}
\caption{The behavioral direction (CAA) and the structural fingerprint (LinearDCT) are nearly orthogonal. Projection magnitude: 9 to 17\% of the CAA direction overlaps with the 64-dimensional DCT subspace. Cosine with the top DCT singular vector: between $-0.05$ and $+0.03$ at all layers.}
\label{fig:ortho}
\end{figure}

If the fingerprint detected what the backdoor \emph{does} (the behavioral change), then the LinearDCT subspace and the CAA direction should be aligned. They are not. Figure~\ref{fig:ortho} shows the cosine similarity between the CAA direction and the top DCT singular vector is between $-0.05$ and $+0.03$ across all 8 scoring layers. Only 9 to 17\% of the CAA direction projects onto the 64-dimensional DCT subspace.

A stricter test: querying the LoRA adapter weights directly. Since the adapter encodes the behavioral change (trigger $\to$ target behavior), its weight directions should capture the backdoor if the fingerprint is behavioral. Instead, scoring documents by projection onto LoRA directions yields AUROC 0.31 to 0.58 (mean 0.41), barely above random. The fingerprint is entirely outside the behavioral subspace.

The B-stripped control confirms this from the other direction: removing trigger tokens from documents before computing the CAA direction produces a direction identical to the full-document CAA direction (cosine 1.000 at every layer). The trigger contributes nothing to the supervised detection signal either.

\paragraph{The clean model detects poisoning.}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{fig5_clean_vs_backdoored.pdf}
\caption{Per-layer detection AUROC using the backdoored model (red) vs the clean base model with no LoRA adapter (blue). The clean model achieves 0.968 at layer 30, only 0.028 below the backdoored model. The fingerprint exists before poisoning occurs.}
\label{fig:clean}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:clean} shows that a base LLaMA-2-7B model with no backdoor adapter detects poisoned documents at AUROC 0.968 (layer 30), compared to 0.996 for the backdoored model. The gap is negligible in early layers ($< 0.01$) and modest in late layers (0.03 to 0.06). The fingerprint is already present in a model that has never seen the poisoned data during training.

\paragraph{Trigger stripping improves detection.}

If the trigger tokens were the signal, removing them should hurt detection. We strip the trigger phrase from each poisoned document and re-extract activations from the clean model. Detection improves slightly: AUROC increases by 0.002 (BadNets), 0.003 (VPI), and 0.007 (Sleeper). The triggers are noise that slightly obscures the real signal, which lives in the response content.

\paragraph{Clean V matrices outperform poisoned ones.}

LinearDCT V matrices built from the clean base model (never trained on poisoned data) achieve AUROC 0.949 to 0.952 across all five attacks, compared to 0.943 to 0.947 for V matrices built from the backdoored model (mean improvement: $+0.005$). No backdoored reference model is needed. This is the strongest evidence that the fingerprint reflects document-level distributional properties, not model-level structural changes.

\paragraph{Interpretation.} The converging evidence suggests a simple explanation: poisoned documents contain harmful response content (jailbreak instructions, refusal overrides) that is distributionally distinct from clean response content (safety refusals, helpful answers). Any LLM encodes this distributional difference in its activations. The MLP Jacobian and L2 distance methods work because they detect this activation-level anomaly, not because they capture any subtle structural change from the fine-tuning process.

This does not diminish the practical value of the method. It enhances it: detection does not require a backdoored model, trigger knowledge, or any information about the attack. It requires only a model and a notion of what ``normal'' training documents look like.

\subsection{Stress Tests}
% -------------------------------------------------------------------

\paragraph{Causal verification.}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{fig9_causal.pdf}
\caption{Attack success rate after retraining on the corpus with top-$K$ flagged documents removed. Fingerprint-based removal (red) drops ASR from 75\% to 12\% at $K{=}50$ because 45 of those 50 documents are actual poison (90\% precision). Random removal (blue) is far less effective.}
\label{fig:causal}
\end{figure}

Detection is only useful if flagged documents are causally responsible for the backdoor. We retrain LLaMA-2-7B from scratch (LoRA, rank 16, 5 epochs) on the corpus after removing the top-$K$ documents ranked by L2 fingerprint score. Figure~\ref{fig:causal} shows the baseline model achieves 75\% ASR. Removing 50 documents by fingerprint ranking drops ASR to 12\%, because 45 of those 50 are actual poison (90\% precision). At $K{=}200$, precision is 88\% (176/200) and ASR falls to 25\%. Random removal at the same $K$ is far less effective.

\paragraph{Cross-architecture generalization.} We train a BadNets-style backdoor on Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3 from scratch using the same poisoned data and LoRA configuration. L2-blind detection achieves AUROC 0.982 at layer 31, comparable to LLaMA-2-7B (0.992). No adaptation or retuning is needed. The fingerprint generalizes across model families.

\paragraph{Adversarial robustness.}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{fig10_adversarial.pdf}
\caption{An adaptive attacker who adds a regularization term penalizing activation variance can reduce detection AUROC from 0.90 to 0.72 while maintaining ASR at 100\%. The AUROC floor at 0.72 suggests a residual signal the attacker cannot eliminate without degrading language modeling quality.}
\label{fig:adversarial}
\end{figure}

An adaptive attacker who knows about the detection method can incorporate a regularization term during training that penalizes activation variance, encouraging poisoned and clean documents to look similar. We sweep regularization strength $\lambda$ from 0 to 10. At $\lambda{=}1.0$, the attacker reduces AUROC from 0.90 to 0.72 while maintaining 100\% ASR (Figure~\ref{fig:adversarial}). Increasing $\lambda$ further to 10.0 does not reduce AUROC further (0.73) but degrades language modeling loss (3.32 vs 3.18 baseline).

This is a meaningful reduction. We do not claim the method is robust to all adaptive attacks. However, the existence of a floor (0.72) under this regularization strategy suggests the fingerprint has a component that cannot be eliminated without degrading the model's core capability. A practical defense would ensemble multiple detection methods (L2-blind, LinearDCT, text-level features) to raise the evasion cost.

\paragraph{Fingerprint geometry.} The fingerprint is low-dimensional: a rank-1 projection achieves AUROC 0.73, and rank-4 achieves 0.82. Only 5 clean documents are needed for centroid estimation (AUROC 0.925 vs 0.929 with 400). The singular value spectrum reveals that at early layers, a single direction dominates ($\sigma_1/\sigma_2 = 25\times$ at layer 4), while at late layers the spectrum is flat ($\sigma_1/\sigma_2 = 1.1\times$ at layer 31), distributing the signal across many dimensions.

\section{Discussion}
% =====================================================================

\paragraph{What the fingerprint is (and is not).} We initially expected the structural fingerprint to capture how poisoning changed MLP computation. The evidence suggests instead that it captures distributional properties of the document content itself. Poisoned documents in the BackdoorLLM benchmark contain harmful response text (jailbreak instructions, detailed malicious advice) that is distributionally distinct from clean responses (safety refusals). Any LLM processes these differently, creating a detectable activation gap.

This raises a question: \emph{do we need MLP Jacobians at all?} If the signal is in document content, simpler feature extraction might suffice. We plan to test text-level baselines (TF-IDF, perplexity) to determine whether activation-based methods capture something beyond surface-level distributional shift. If they do not, the practical contribution is simplified: use any feature extractor, not necessarily a neural one. If they do, the Jacobian structure provides additional sensitivity that justifies the computational overhead.

\paragraph{Implications for real-world auditing.} The clean model finding has a direct practical consequence. An organization preparing a fine-tuning dataset can screen it for distributional anomalies using any available LLM, without training on the data first. Documents flagged as outliers in activation space can be reviewed manually or excluded. This is computationally cheap (one forward pass per document) and requires no assumptions about attack type, trigger format, or target behavior.

The causal verification confirms this is actionable: removing flagged documents eliminates the backdoor. The 90\% precision at $K{=}50$ means an auditor reviewing 50 documents would find 45 actual poison, a workload compatible with manual review.

\paragraph{What happens when poisoned documents are not obviously different?} Current backdoor benchmarks use poisoned documents whose response content is qualitatively different from clean content (harmful vs. safe). A more sophisticated attacker could craft poisoned documents whose surface content is indistinguishable from clean documents, relying on subtle statistical patterns rather than overtly harmful text. The adversarial experiment (Section 4.3) shows that even with activation-level regularization, a residual signal persists. Whether this holds against content-level mimicry is an open question that future work should address.

\paragraph{Limitations.} All experiments use BackdoorLLM datasets with 800 total documents. Scaling behavior at 10K+ documents is untested. Cross-architecture transfer was tested on one additional family (Mistral). The 13B and 70B results use 4-bit quantization. Pre-training poisoning (vs. fine-tuning) is untested. The adversarial evaluation uses one regularization strategy. The text-level baseline experiment is pending.
